## 6. Motors, types and Operation
https://electronics.howstuffworks.com/motor.htm
https://www.iqsdirectory.com/articles/electric-motor/ac-motor.html

Electric motors convert electrical energy into mechanical motion, and their design choices are driven by source waveform, speed range, precision, torque profile, efficiency, and the amount of control electronics acceptable in the system. Classification often depends on whether the power source is DC, AC, or pulsed DC, whether torque is produced by electromagnetic force, magnetic reluctance, or direct linear force, whether commutation is mechanical, electronic, or absent, and whether control is open-loop, closed-loop, or variable-frequency.

### 6.1 Brushed DC Motor
![[basics-of-brushed-dc-motors-img1.jpg]]
A brushed DC motor consists of a stator that is usually a permanent magnet and a rotor, typically an armature that  
generates torque through the Lorentz force ($\vec{F}=I\,\vec{L}\times\vec{B}$), as direct current flows through armature conductors that lie within a stationary magnetic field. A segmented commutator reverses each coil’s current every $180^{\circ}$ of rotation so the torque remains unidirectional. Electrically, the armature is characterized by resistance $R_a$ and a back-EMF $E_b$ proportional to angular speed ($E_b = k_e \omega$), while torque $T$ is proportional to armature current ($T = k_t I_a$). 

This gives the motor an approximately linear torque-speed relationship at constant field flux. Construction includes a stator made from permanent magnets or field windings, a laminated iron armature core with coils, and a copper commutator with carbon brushes forming sliding electrical contact. Brushed DC motors are simple to drive because voltage controls speed and current controls torque, they deliver good low-speed torque, and they respond quickly to current changes. Their main drawbacks are brush and commutator wear, arcing that causes EMI and dust, and lower efficiency compared to brushless designs. They are common in automotive starters, power tools, toys, small actuators, and low-cost winches.

### 6.2 Brushless DC Motor (BLDC)
A brushless DC motor removes the mechanical commutator by fixing permanent magnets to the rotor and using electronic commutation in the stator. Rotor position is detected by Hall sensors, back-EMF, or encoders so the controller can sequence current through the stator coils and produce a rotating magnetic field. Torque is proportional to current and the rotor’s angular position relative to that field; back-EMF is used in sensorless designs for commutation and speed estimation. The stator is a laminated core with concentrated windings arranged in three or six phases, while the rotor contains high-energy magnets that may be surface-mounted or interior-mounted. 

An ESC consisting of MOSFETs or IGBTs switches the phase currents. BLDC motors are valued for high efficiency, high power density, good thermal behavior because the stator is the heat source, and the ability to run at high RPMs without brush friction. The tradeoff is that they require dedicated drive electronics, low-speed startup is more difficult without sensors, and their initial cost is higher than brushed DC. They are widely used in drones, cooling fans, electric bicycles, premium power tools, and EV-related systems.

### 6.3 Servo Motor
A servo motor is an integrated motion system that combines a motor with a sensor, gearbox, and controller to achieve precise position, speed, or torque control. The closed-loop system compares the commanded output to the actual output and regulates motor current to minimize error. Typical architecture includes a command input via PWM or digital communication, a feedback device such as an encoder or potentiometer, a controller using PID or vector control, and a power stage that drives the motor through a gearbox or direct coupling. Servo systems can use brushed DC, BLDC, or AC synchronous/induction motors, but the key attribute is the feedback loop. They offer high accuracy, repeatability, the ability to respond dynamically to load changes, and strong holding capability. Their complexity and cost are greater than open-loop systems, and they require careful tuning to avoid overshoot or oscillation. Servo packages are essential in industrial robotics, CNC axes, camera gimbals, antenna positioning, and aerospace control actuation.

### 6.4 Stepper Motor
A stepper motor advances in fixed angular increments when stator phases are energized in sequence, making it suitable for open-loop positioning if the load stays within torque limits. Permanent-magnet steppers use magnetic rotors, variable-reluctance steppers use soft iron rotors, and hybrid steppers combine permanent magnets with toothed rotors for the best accuracy and torque. Control modes include full step, half step, and microstepping; microstepping smooths motion by dividing current between phases to create fractional steps and reduce vibration. Built from a stator with finely toothed poles and phase windings and a magnetized or toothed rotor, stepper motors provide reliable holding torque at zero speed and controlled resolution based on pole count and driver approach. Their limitations are step loss under overload or rapid acceleration, constant current draw while holding, and resonance or noise at certain step rates. Steppers are popular in 3D printers, CNC machines, camera sliders, textile equipment, and laboratory automation.

### 6.5 Squirrel Cage Induction Motor
A squirrel cage induction motor is a three-phase AC machine where the stator produces a rotating magnetic field at synchronous speed $\omega_s = 2\pi f / p$, with $f$ the line frequency and $p$ the number of pole pairs. The rotating field induces currents in the rotor bars; the resulting rotor field attempts to follow the stator field and thus rotates slightly slower, with that difference called slip. Torque is proportional to slip at low speeds and peaks at a characteristic slip determined by rotor impedance. Its robust construction uses a laminated stator core with distributed phase windings and a rotor made from copper or aluminum bars cast into an iron core and shorted by end rings. The design is simple, self-starting, and tolerant of harsh environments, but it offers poor speed regulation unless paired with a VFD, and it draws a large inrush current during startup. This makes it the lowest cost-per-horsepower industrial workhorse for pumps, fans, compressors, conveyors, blowers, and constant-speed manufacturing equipment.

### 6.6 Slip-Ring Induction Motor (Wound Rotor)
A slip-ring induction motor resembles a squirrel cage machine except the rotor has insulated windings brought through slip rings to an external circuit. By inserting resistance or electronic control into the rotor circuit during startup, the torque-speed characteristic can be reshaped to provide strong starting torque and gentler acceleration while limiting current. At low speed, high external resistance shifts maximum torque toward startup; as the rotor accelerates, the resistance is reduced so the motor transitions to standard running performance. The stator uses the same three-phase distributed winding, while the rotor and slip-ring assembly add complexity and maintenance requirements. Slip-ring motors are ideal when heavy loads must start under full load and when controlled acceleration is needed, but they are more expensive and require brush-and-ring upkeep. They are commonly used in cranes, mills, conveyors, hoists, crushers, and large pumps.

### 6.7 Linear Motor
A linear motor converts electrical power directly into linear force by generating a traveling magnetic field along a straight path. It is conceptually an unrolled rotary motor, with the primary acting as a linear stator and the secondary as a moving carriage. Linear induction motors induce currents in a secondary plate, linear synchronous motors use permanent magnets and a synchronized traveling field, and linear DC motors produce motion in a straight geometry analogous to rotary DC designs. Construction includes an active primary coil assembly, a secondary moving plate or magnet assembly, and guidance systems such as rails, bearings, or magnetic levitation. The result is direct drive without mechanical transmission, offering high acceleration and precise motion control. The downsides are high cost for long travel distances, thermal management needs, and the requirement for clean environments because stray ferrous debris can be attracted. Linear motors are used in maglev trains, semiconductor wafer steppers, pick-and-place machinery, and precision automation.

### 6.8 Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motor (PMSM)
A permanent magnet synchronous motor is an AC machine whose rotor contains permanent magnets, and the stator is driven with controlled three-phase sinusoidal currents to produce a rotating field. The rotor locks to that field and turns at synchronous speed, so there is effectively no slip at steady state. Surface-mounted PM motors have magnets on the rotor face, while interior PM motors bury magnets inside the rotor to provide both magnet torque and reluctance torque. Field-Oriented Control (FOC) decomposes stator currents into torque-producing and flux-producing components, maximizing efficiency and torque density. PMSMs are prized for top-tier efficiency, smooth torque delivery, and excellent performance across wide speed ranges, but they depend on expensive rare-earth magnets and precise control electronics, and the magnets can demagnetize if overheated or exposed to adverse flux. They power electric vehicles, industrial servo drives, HVAC compressors, and renewable energy generators.

### 6.9 Switched Reluctance Motor (SRM)
A switched reluctance motor generates torque by energizing stator phases so the rotor teeth align with the active poles, minimizing magnetic reluctance. The rotor is a simple laminated steel assembly with no magnets, wires, or currents, so torque is produced only by the changing reluctance of the air gap. SRM drives require precise phase timing and current profiling to shape torque and reduce ripple, and modern digital controllers are essential to manage acoustic noise. The motor is extremely robust, thermally tolerant, and fault-resistant, but it is also inherently noisy and vibratory without advanced control compensation. SRMs are often chosen for demanding industrial machinery, automotive pumps, compressors, and aerospace actuators where ruggedness and reliability outweigh the need for low noise.

### 6.10 Engineering Selection Matrix
A concise engineering comparison highlights the tradeoffs between motor types. Brushed DC motors are simple DC machines with mechanical brushes and a straightforward voltage-to-torque relationship, but they suffer from brush wear and arcing. BLDC motors use electronic commutation and offer high efficiency and RPM, yet they depend on controller complexity and can be challenging to start sensorlessly. Servo motors combine closed-loop feedback with precision control, making them ideal for accurate motion, but they demand careful tuning and may introduce gearbox issues. Stepper motors provide repeatable incremental motion without feedback, while their vulnerability lies in step loss and resonance. Squirrel cage induction motors are low-cost and rugged on three-phase AC, but their fixed speed and high inrush current can be limiting. Slip-ring motors offer controlled high starting torque on three-phase AC at the cost of slip-ring maintenance. Linear motors deliver backlash-free direct motion, but they carry thermal and magnetic cost penalties. PMSMs provide superior efficiency and power density with field-oriented control, yet they are sensitive to magnet temperature. SRMs are rugged and high-speed on pulsed DC, but they must manage torque ripple and noise carefully.
