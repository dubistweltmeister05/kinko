- I picked a niche that wasn't massively talked about

I am a firmware engineer by profession, and I have had a keen interest in electronics. I've been into it since I was in college, and I have always wanted to make a career around it. Now, when I was online, searching for help and resources about it, there were little to none, so I swore to myself that if I ever make it, I'll gladly fill in that gap.

And thus, this account's existence came into fruition. I kept talking about electronics, automotive, robotics, embedded systems and software, low-level programming, engineering education in India, my internships, and so much more.

What helped massively here was that I wasn't competing in an oversaturated arena where thousands of people were saying the exact same thing every single day. Most people online gravitate toward already popular niches because they assume audience density equals growth. But what actually happens is that you become one more voice in an already deafening crowd.

When you operate in a relatively underrepresented domain, even mediocre consistency gives you visibility because there simply aren't enough people talking about those things regularly. Over time, people start associating your account with that field itself. You become mentally indexed into their heads. Whenever they think "embedded systems",  or "electronics engineering",  your account appears somewhere in their memory because you repeatedly occupied that space.

Another thing that helped was that I wasn't just reposting surface-level information. I was talking from lived experience. There is a huge difference between a person summarizing Wikipedia pages and a person speaking after actually debugging hardware at 2AM, frying boards accidentally, struggling through internships, dealing with bad documentation, understanding engineering culture, and navigating career confusion. Authenticity compounds heavily online because people can subconsciously detect whether you actually live the things you speak about.

And because engineering is such a broad ecosystem, one topic naturally flowed into another. Embedded systems led into Linux. Linux led into tooling. Tooling led into software architecture. Automotive led into systems safety. Internships led into engineering education discussions. Suddenly, the account had depth instead of just being "another tech account."

- The next point would be that I followed a central theme, but wasn't bound to it.

No doubt, I am an electronics guy. But that isn't what I posted about because there's so much more to me as a human man. I posted guitar covers, football tweets, football match stats of myself from each game that I played in, Basketball tweets, general memes, college day horrors and stories from my degree days, insecurities about life, shifting to Surat, resigning, being unemployed for 3 weeks, finding offers, moving back to Pune, life at my new org, resigning from said org, to joining a new startup, meetups, experiences, throwbacks, political opinions, startups, Linux, software engineering, and a few bait posts.

So my next suggestion, would be that you shouldn't be chained to your niche. Keep it central to your account, sure, but don't let it be your only identity. Be human guys, and you'll naturally attract humans to you!

Man, ISTG - a lot of people misunderstand personal branding and reduce themselves into content machines. Every post becomes optimized, polished, sanitized, hyper-strategized and artificially aligned to a niche. The problem is that human beings do not emotionally connect to niches. They connect to personalities.

Someone may initially follow you because of firmware engineering, but they stay because they start recognizing you as a real person with real experiences, failures, emotions, hobbies, opinions and flaws. That humanization process matters immensely online. It creates familiarity. Familiarity creates attachment. Attachment creates long-term audience retention.

People do not remember isolated information. They remember narratives. They remember "that electronics guy who also played football and kept posting insane internship stories." They remember "that Linux guy who used to talk about being unemployed and confused." Those little unrelated details make your account memorable because they create dimensionality.

Ironically, showing non-niche aspects of your life often strengthens your niche authority instead of weakening it. Why? Because it makes people trust you more. You stop looking like an engagement-farming content account and start looking like an actual engineer living an actual life.

And frankly speaking, constantly restricting yourself to one topic becomes mentally exhausting. If you force yourself into a hyper-narrow identity online, eventually you begin resenting your own account because it stops representing you.

- Articulate yourself well

This isn't rocket science. Write well, and you'll attract more people who want to read what you have written, or hear what you have to say!

Most people heavily underestimate how important communication is online. You can have incredible ideas, insane technical knowledge, and years of experience, but if your writing is dry, confusing, incoherent or emotionally flat, very few people will stick around long enough to understand what you're trying to say.

Good articulation is not about using complicated words or sounding intellectual. In fact, the smartest communicators usually simplify things aggressively. The real skill lies in being able to take thoughts floating around in your head and translating them into words that create clarity, emotion and imagery inside someone else's head.

People often say "this person is smart" when what they actually mean is "this person explains things clearly." That distinction matters a lot. Communication creates perceived intelligence online far more than raw intelligence itself.

Another thing people fail to realize is that writing style itself becomes an identity marker. Some people are analytical. Some are humorous. Some are aggressive. Some are reflective. Some are poetic. Over time, people begin recognizing your writing even before they see your username. That level of identity formation is extremely powerful.

And honestly, articulation also determines how shareable your content becomes. If a tweet communicates a feeling or thought more effectively than what someone could've written themselves, they'll repost it because you articulated what they already felt but couldn't express properly. That is half the game on this app.

- Originality and Individuality in your thought

People are attracted to ideas, you see. And the more original you are in your thought, the more you will attract people. Have thoughts that can make someone think too, and you'll instantly earn a follower in someone. That is the simplest way to grow on this app. Make people stop scrolling, and think about what you have written/said.

Most people online simply remix consensus opinions. They observe what already performs well, slightly modify the wording, and repost it in a different format. The issue is that this creates content that is algorithmically optimized but mentally disposable. Nobody remembers it because there was no actual thought embedded into it.

Originality doesn't necessarily mean inventing entirely new concepts. It simply means arriving at observations through your own lived experiences and your own internal reasoning instead of copying the emotional tone and worldview of the timeline around you.

Whenever you write something genuinely thoughtful, people can feel it. There is a noticeable difference between "manufactured engagement bait" and "a person genuinely thinking out loud." The second category builds stronger audiences because it attracts people intellectually instead of merely farming reactions emotionally.

The tweets that changed my growth trajectory the most were often not the most polished or technically detailed ones. They were the ones where I expressed an unusual perspective, connected unrelated ideas together, challenged a common assumption, or articulated something people quietly felt but never verbalized themselves.

The internet rewards novelty heavily because novelty interrupts attention. If your thought process feels predictable, people subconsciously classify your content as background noise. But if you consistently produce perspectives that feel fresh, insightful, or slightly unconventional, people begin following you because they become curious about how you think.

And honestly, curiosity is one of the strongest reasons anyone follows another human being online.

- Spaces are your biggest advantage.

Trust me, I still remember the spaces by NeuralNets and the magic that used to happen there, deep in the night, as we spoke and spoke till 2-3AM every damn day! It was all AI themed, and I used to be that one guy always talking about "hardware perspective" in AI. I'll be honest, I used to larp a little bit in there, not completely knowing half the things I was speaking about. But I was a voice that intrigued peeps, and I spoke quite well, so that helped me a TON.

Spaces are massively underrated because they accelerate familiarity far faster than tweets ever can. A tweet lets people read your thoughts. Your voice lets people experience your personality. Those are two completely different things.

The moment people hear you speak confidently, passionately, emotionally or intelligently about something, they begin forming a much stronger psychological connection to you. Suddenly, you stop being a profile picture and become an actual human presence in their minds.

And the funny thing is, perfection matters way less in Spaces than people think. A lot of growth comes simply from participation. Most listeners aren't expecting some Harvard-level lecture. They're looking for energy, conviction, authenticity, perspective and conversational ability.

A huge amount of networking on this app also happens indirectly through repeated exposure in these spaces. People hear you speak regularly, recognize your voice, begin seeing your tweets more often, and eventually follow you almost subconsciously because familiarity has already been established beforehand.

Another underrated aspect is that Spaces sharpen articulation skills aggressively. You cannot hide behind edited drafts while speaking live. You learn how to think in real-time, explain concepts spontaneously, navigate disagreements, handle uncertainty, and structure thoughts quickly. Those skills eventually improve your writing too.

And frankly, some of the strongest online friendships, collaborations and opportunities happen in these random late-night conversations where people are simply talking freely without trying too hard to optimize for content.

---

- Videos

Yeah, in the new algo, these are what will boost your followers. Trust me on that.

Attention spans online are collapsing rapidly, and platforms are aggressively prioritizing content formats that maximize watch time. Video naturally dominates in this environment because it combines visuals, audio, emotion, pacing and personality simultaneously.

A well-made video communicates more information in 30 seconds than multiple tweets often can. People can hear your tone, see your expressions, understand your energy levels, observe your confidence, and process visual context all at once. That creates a much stronger viewer impression.

For technical creators specifically, videos are an enormous advantage because engineering concepts become dramatically easier to explain visually. Showing hardware, debugging sessions, prototypes, setups, robotics projects, embedded boards, oscilloscopes, simulations or workflows instantly makes your content more engaging than pure text alone.

And honestly, videos humanize you extremely fast. Many people who appear "cold" or overly technical in writing suddenly become approachable the moment others see them speak casually on camera.

The barrier to entry is also much lower than people assume. Most viewers care far more about clarity, authenticity and insight than cinematic perfection. Some of the highest-performing videos online are literally just smart people explaining interesting things passionately with decent lighting and clear audio.

The algorithm may push the video initially, but what retains followers afterward is still the same thing as always: whether you made people feel informed, entertained, thoughtful or emotionally connected.

---

And honestly, I think the biggest takeaway people should extract from all this is that growth online is rarely about "gaming the algorithm" as much as people think. The algorithm only amplifies signals that already exist. If you're genuinely interesting, articulate, thoughtful, emotionally expressive, skilled at something, or deeply involved in a domain, eventually people notice.

Most creators fail because they optimize for growth before they optimize for becoming worth following in the first place. The people who grow sustainably are usually the people who are simply documenting real interests, real struggles, real experiences and real thoughts consistently over long periods of time.

Another thing people should understand is that audience-building compounds slowly and then suddenly. For a long time, it feels like nobody cares. Then one day, years of accumulated identity, writing style, reputation, relationships, ideas and consistency begin stacking together simultaneously. People discover you through one tweet, then stay because your entire account already feels alive and layered.

And lastly, don't over-manufacture yourself online. The internet already has enough optimized personalities. What people remember are distinct humans.
